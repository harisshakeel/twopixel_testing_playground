## District at a Glance

**Downingtown Area School District** is a regular local school district located in PA. The district serves approximately **13,204 students** across **16 schools** in a **Suburb: Large** setting (NCES data, 2022-23).

## Academic Programs & Intervention Systems

The Downingtown Area School District (DASD) has established a comprehensive and structured system for academic programs and student interventions, anchored by a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) and supported by detailed curriculum review cycles, specialized programs, and robust assessment practices. The district’s instructional vision emphasizes rigorous, challenging curriculum and instruction, engaging learning activities, and empowering student agency.

### MTSS/RTI Framework
DASD has implemented a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) as a district-wide initiative to identify and support struggling students. The framework is a three-tiered process. Universal screening of all students occurs three times per year using assessments such as **Aimsweb+** and **i-Ready Diagnostic**. This screening identifies students as performing at grade level (Benchmark/Tier 1), slightly below grade level (Strategic/Tier 2), or well below grade level (Intensive/Tier 3). Following screening, an instructional plan is implemented for each student. Tier 2 and Tier 3 students receive additional teacher-guided instruction in small groups, which may be delivered by teachers other than their homeroom teacher while they continue with regular classroom instruction. These students are progress monitored during the intervention period to measure growth. Decisions to move a student between tiers, continue the current intervention, or change the intervention program are based solely on the collected progress monitoring data. The district emphasizes parental involvement, with communication to parents/guardians deemed necessary to ensure student success.

### Intervention Programs and Small Group Instruction
The MTSS process drives the formation of Tier 2 and Tier 3 small-group intervention groups. These groups are formed based on data from the tri-annual screening assessments. The district uses specific programs to deliver these interventions, though the core content pages primarily reference the screening and progress monitoring tools rather than naming specific intervention curricula. For mathematics, the new **i-Ready Classroom Mathematics** program, launching in the 2025-2026 school year, includes integrated MTSS and intervention support, suggesting that intervention strategies are embedded within the core curricular resource. The **i-Ready** platform itself provides differentiated digital mathematics instruction with adaptive pathways to address unique student needs. In reading, the high school offers a tiered program for students who continue to struggle, with placement decisions made by the DASD Reading Department based on various benchmark and diagnostic reading assessments. The formation of groups appears fluid, as students may move between tiers or change intervention plans based on progress monitoring data reviewed after a specific intervention period.

### Progress Monitoring
Progress monitoring is a formal component of the MTSS process for Tier 2 and Tier 3 students. The tools used for this monitoring are not explicitly named in the general MTSS description but are implied to be connected to the screening assessment system (e.g., **Aimsweb+**). The frequency of monitoring is described as occurring "over the course of the intervention plan." The results of this progress monitoring are reviewed during and after the intervention to determine its success and to inform all subsequent decisions about a student's tier placement or instructional plan.

### Special Education Services
DASD provides a full continuum of special education services in accordance with federal and state law, with a mission to educate all students in the least restrictive environment. The district develops Individualized Education Programs (IEPs) to provide specially designed instruction, related services, and access to the general curriculum. A student is eligible for an IEP if they meet criteria for one of 13 educational disability categories under PA law *and* require specific instruction from a special education teacher to make adequate educational progress. The district offers a range of specialized classroom placements at the elementary level. Autistic Support classrooms are located at Beaver Creek, Bradford Heights, Brandywine Wallace, Lionville, Pickering Valley, Springton Manor, and Uwchlan Hills Elementary Schools. Life Skills Support classrooms are at West Bradford and Pickering Valley Elementary. A Multiple Disabilities Support classroom is at Pickering Valley Elementary. An Emotional Support classroom is at Shamona Creek Elementary, and a Language Support classroom is at East Ward Elementary. The district has also revised its policy to align with a PDE change, now providing a Free and Appropriate Public Education to eligible students until their 22nd birthday.

### Gifted Education
Gifted education services are provided to students in elementary, middle, and high school through Gifted Individualized Education Plans (GIEPs) that offer enrichment, acceleration, or a combination of both. The identification process is a multi-tiered, multiple-criteria system. Parents may request screening at any time, and the district also conducts a Universal Screening process using benchmark data. For grades K-6, Phase 1 uses the **i-Ready** ELA and math diagnostic assessments. Phase 2 administers the **Screening Assessment for Gifted Elementary and Middle School Students (SAGES-3)**. Phase 3 involves parent/teacher input and individualized cognitive (IQ) testing by a School Psychologist. For grades 7-8, Phase 2 includes both the **SAGES-3** and the **Classroom Diagnostic Tool (CDT)**. For grades 9-12, Phase 1 uses standardized ELA and math assessments, Phase 2 uses the **CDT**, and Phase 3 includes parent/teacher input and IQ testing. If a student is found eligible, a GIEP is developed and reviewed annually.

### Section 504 Services
Eligibility for a 504 Service Agreement requires a student to have a physical or mental disability that substantially limits one or more major life activities and requires accommodations to have equal access to the regular education program. The district provides procedural safeguards for parents and distinguishes 504 plans from IEPs, noting that a 504 plan provides accommodations within the regular classroom, while an IEP requires specially designed instruction from a special education teacher.

### Title I Program
Title I is a federally funded supplemental education program. In DASD, four elementary schools receive these funds for targeted assistance to support math instruction for eligible K-5 students: Beaver Creek, Bradford Heights, East Ward, and Lionville Elementary. Eligibility for extra math support is determined using **Aimsweb Plus** and **iReady** assessments. Parents of qualifying students are invited to participate and complete a Home/School Involvement Compact. The program is supported by math specialists, and parents are encouraged to join a Parent Advisory Board to provide input on the Title I Parent & Family Engagement Plan for their building. The district also runs a Preschool Outreach Program that partners with local preschools to offer monthly educational activities and informational sessions for families preparing for kindergarten.

### Early Intervention and Transition
The district has a structured transition process for incoming kindergarten students who receive services from the Chester County Intermediate Unit (CCIU) Early Intervention program. The process involves a release of information, a meet-and-greet transition meeting with the district team and CCIU, kindergarten registration, and a letter of intent from the district regarding the child's IEP. For students aging out of the K-12 system, DASD offers extensive Post Secondary Transition Services beginning at age 14, focusing on education/training, employment, and independent living. The district operates a Transition Program for qualified students ages 18-21, which includes a transition home for authentic living experiences and a DASD Work Experience Program. This program provides pre-vocational training and community job coaching with local business partners to help students generalize vocational skills.

### Curriculum and Instructional Approach
DASD’s curriculum is rooted in Pennsylvania Department of Education standards and managed through a systematic six-stage Curriculum Review Cycle. The stages are Research and Audit, Develop, Implement Year 1, Implement Year 2, Monitor, and Evaluate. Each curricular area progresses through this cycle on a rotating schedule. The district is transitioning its curriculum documents to an online platform called **Chalk**. A significant recent curriculum adoption is **i-Ready Classroom Mathematics** for grades K-8, beginning in the 2025-2026 school year. This program was selected after a year-long review process involving teacher pilots, site visits, and an evaluation rubric. The program emphasizes conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, and real-world problem-solving through an instructional framework called Try–Discuss–Connect. It is designed to integrate with the district’s MTSS and assessment systems. For grades 9-12 mathematics, the district piloted resources in 2023-2024 and subsequently selected **Big Ideas Math: Modeling Real Life (2025 copyright)** for implementation in the 2025-2026 school year. In English Language Arts, a review for grades 6-8 is underway for the 2024-2027 cycle, with pilots of **Amplify ELA** and a to-be-determined second resource (**SAVVAS My Perspectives** or **McGraw Hill StudySync**) scheduled for early 2025, leading to a new resource implementation in the 2026-2027 school year.

### Assessment and Grading Practices
The district administers state-mandated **PSSA** tests in grades 3-8 (ELA and Math) and grades 5 & 8 (Science), and **Keystone Exams** in Algebra I, Literature, and Biology for high school students. Keystone proficiency is a graduation requirement for the class of 2023 and beyond, with alternate pathways available under Act 158. Locally, DASD uses a standards-based grading (SBG) system for elementary (K-5) report cards. Students are assessed on their progress toward mastering PA Core Standards using four Indicators of Success: 4 (Exceeds Standard), 3 (Meets Standard), 2 (Approaching Standard), and 1 (Limited Progress to Standard). Proficiency is determined through a body of evidence including common summative assessments, formative assessments, classwork, and teacher observations. Expectations for meeting standards differ by subject; ELA standards are typically end-of-year expectations, while Math, Science, and Social Studies standards are often end-of-unit or during-the-year expectations. Families access report cards via the Infinite Campus Parent Portal.

### English Language Development (ELD)
Students are screened for the English Language Development Program using the **WIDA Screener**. Qualifying students receive instruction in listening, speaking, reading, and writing from an ELD teacher. At the elementary level, this is typically a 30-minute daily pull-out model. In grades 6-12, students may receive direct English instruction and/or content support. All English Learners take the annual **WIDA ACCESS** test to measure growth toward proficiency. Students who meet exit criteria are monitored for two years. The district’s Language Instruction Educational Program (LIEP) outlines the plan for identifying, serving, and monitoring ELs.

### Student Achievement and Performance Trends
According to the 2024-2025 Student Achievement Report, DASD demonstrates strong academic performance. In mathematics, proficiency rates increased in grades 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, and 11, with grade 5 ranking first in Chester County for proficiency. The district’s percentile ranks in the state are at or above the 90th percentile for grades 3-6 and 11 in math. Growth metrics, as measured by PVAAS, show grade 4 and Algebra I had the highest growth in the state. In ELA/Literature, proficiency increased in grades 6 and 11, and the district holds top-three proficiency ranks in Chester County for grades 3-5 and 8. For Biology, proficiency has increased over two years, placing DASD fourth in the county, and academic growth continues to be well above state expectations. The district’s STEM Academy holds the highest biology and literature proficiency rates in Chester County. These results indicate effective implementation of current programs and a focus on continuous improvement through the curriculum review cycle.

## Mental Health, Wellness & Student Support

The Downingtown Area School District (DASD) maintains a comprehensive, multi-tiered system of mental health, wellness, and student support services explicitly aligned with a "whole child" philosophy. The district's commitment is formalized through a 20-year-old wellness initiative, a district-wide wellness plan, and integration into the 2024-2030 Strategic Plan. This infrastructure is designed to increase protective factors, decrease risk factors, and support the academic, social-emotional, and post-secondary success of all 13,563 students.

### Counseling and Clinical Staff Infrastructure
DASD employs a substantial team of dedicated mental health professionals distributed across all grade levels. The district staffs 37 school counselors, 13 prevention specialists, 15 school psychologists, 2 home and school visitors, 1 elementary behavior specialist, and 1 student services supervisor. Elementary school counselors are assigned to each of the district's 10 elementary schools, with responsibilities including individual and group counseling using solution-focused brief counseling (SFBC) methods, peer conflict resolution, teaching social-emotional learning curriculum, and serving on building Crisis Response Teams. At the secondary level, including the STEM Academy, counseling departments are structured by student last name to manage academic advising, social-emotional support, and post-secondary planning. Counselors operate under an "open door" policy, encouraging students to seek support without an appointment.

### Multi-Tiered Systems of Support and Early Intervention
The district utilizes several research-based frameworks for early identification and tiered support. **Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS)** is implemented as a school-wide system. **Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS)** are used for the early identification of behavioral support needs. The cornerstone intervention program is the **Student Assistance Program (SAP)**, a mandated team-based process available to all students in grades 6-12. SAP teams, composed of trained faculty, are designed to assist in identifying issues including alcohol, tobacco, other drugs, and mental health concerns which pose a barrier to a student's success. The process involves referral, data collection, team review, and, when warranted, a recommendation for a screening or assessment by a community mental health or drug and alcohol liaison. The district emphasizes that SAP is not for crisis situations and is not a treatment program.

### Mental Health Screening and Assessment Practices
DASD employs a variety of screening tools for behavioral and mental health. The district conducts suicide screenings and assessments for all students in grades K-12. It also administers **Review 360 student screenings**, though the specific focus of this tool is not detailed. At the elementary level, schools report using a **Behavioral and Emotional Screener** as part of their wellness strategies. The district also actively participates in the **Pennsylvania Youth Survey (PAYS)**, a biennial survey administered to students in grades 6, 8, 10, and 12 to assess risk and protective factors related to substance use and mental health. In the 2023 administration, 3,317 DASD students participated. Data from PAYS is used to inform wellness goals and community prevention efforts, with noted improvements such as a decrease from 32% to 25% of students reporting feeling depressed and sad most days.

### Social-Emotional Learning and Prevention Curriculum
A structured SEL curriculum is delivered across all grade levels. In elementary school (K-5), school counselors teach dedicated classroom lessons. The district has adopted the **Second Step SEL Curriculum** for elementary grades. For grades 6-10, mental health lessons have been added to the health curriculum, with active involvement from school counselors. A critical component of the secondary health curriculum is the **Signs of Suicide (SOS) program**, an evidence-based suicide prevention program taught in grades 6-10 health classes. This program educates students on recognizing the signs of depression and suicide in themselves and their peers and how to seek help.

### Crisis Response and Safety Protocols
The district maintains robust, clearly communicated 24-hour crisis support options for students and families. These include the national **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**, the local **Valley Creek Crisis Intervention** hotline (610-280-3270), and the **Safe2Say Something** anonymous tip line for reporting concerns about self-harm or threats. All school websites prominently display these resources. School counselors act as members of building-level Crisis Response Teams. Furthermore, the district has a formal policy (Policy 823) regarding the maintenance and administration of **Naloxone** for emergency use in cases of suspected opioid overdose to enhance the health and safety of students, staff, and visitors.

### Wellness Initiatives and Whole Child Approach
Wellness is a deeply embedded, district-wide priority coordinated by a Wellness Advisory Committee and implemented by building-level wellness teams in all 16 schools. For the 2024-2025 school year, these teams reported 123 fully implemented wellness strategies impacting both students and staff. The district's wellness goals are to increase protective factors and decrease risk factors associated with substance use and mental health; sustain a healthy environment for eating and physical activity; promote staff health and wellness; and support families in meeting youth wellness needs. This work is explicitly framed around the **Whole School, Whole Community, Whole Child (WSCC)** model from the CDC and ASCD, ensuring attention to the tenets of health, safety, engagement, support, and challenge. The district's "Portrait of a Graduate" also includes a priority on health and wellness, expecting graduates to make informed choices to promote healthy lifestyles and relationships.

### Student-Led Clubs and Peer Support
The district fosters peer-to-peer support and advocacy through several student-led clubs. **Healthy Youth Positive Energy (HYPE)** clubs operate in grades 6-12 across the middle schools, high schools, and STEM Academy, with 163 total participants in the 2024-2025 year. These student-driven clubs engage in advocacy and awareness activities for substance use prevention and mental health, such as Red Ribbon Week, World Suicide Prevention Day, and organizing wellness events. Other supportive clubs include **Aevidum**, focused on mental health awareness, and various elementary school "buddy" programs and mindfulness clubs. Some schools also host "Zen Den" spaces and utilize therapy animal visits, such as from "To Love A Canine."

### Community Partnerships and Collaborative Networks
DASD maintains extensive partnerships with local and county organizations to augment its mental health infrastructure. Key partners include **Communities That Care of Greater Downingtown (CTC)**, which co-hosts the Parent/Caregiver Speaker Series, oversees the HYPE clubs, and produces the "Parent to Parent" podcast and blog. **Cartwheel** provides a series of supporting family wellness and mental health webinars for the community. **Caron** treatment centers are listed as a resource. The district also collaborates with county systems, referencing **Chester County Child & Adolescent Mental Health Services** and the **Chester County Maternal and Child Health Consortium**. The involvement of prevention specialists and references to SAP liaisons indicate alignment with the regional **Pennsylvania Network for Student Assistance Services (PNSAS)** and county-level behavioral health systems for integrated service delivery.

### Parent and Family Education and Support
The district invests significantly in parent and caregiver education through its **Parent/Caregiver Speaker Series**, a collaborative effort with CTC. This series offers in-person and recorded webinar events on topics such as vaping, screen time, school avoidance, neuroscience of high-risk behavior, and underage drinking prevention (e.g., MADD's Power of Parents program). In the 2024-2025 school year, 300 parents attended or viewed recordings of these events. CTC also hosts monthly "Parent to Parent" group meetings and a podcast with over 6,870 total listens. Past speaker series recordings are archived on the DASD YouTube Pupil Services playlist, providing ongoing access to resources on mental health, substance use, technology, and parenting strategies.

### Staff Wellness Program
The district operates a long-standing staff wellness initiative called **Living Well @ DASD**, with a mission to enhance the mental, social, emotional, and physical well-being of staff. The program is managed by Human Resources and a district-level staff wellness committee. Key offerings include an annual **DASD Walking Challenge** (404 participants in 2024-2025), a **4-Week Healthy Habits Challenge**, flu shot clinics, and promotion of the **Health Advocate** Employee Assistance Program which provides 8 free counseling sessions per issue. The district also provides access to **SwiftMD/Revive Health**, a telemedicine service. Building-level wellness liaisons facilitate additional activities such as staff fitness clubs, walking groups, pickleball tournaments, healthy recipe shares, and recognition events. These efforts are formally tracked as part of the district's wellness goals and action plans.

### Home, School, and Community Liaison Services
The district employs two **Home and School Visitors** (school social workers) who hold advanced degrees and certifications. Their role is to support students and families in the removal of barriers to education, including crisis intervention, referrals to outside resources, support for transient families, addressing poor attendance, and providing professional development for staff on topics like trauma-informed education. They serve as a critical bridge between home and school, particularly for students experiencing social, emotional, or environmental challenges.

### Measurement and Data-Driven Practices
The district utilizes several forms of data to guide its mental health and wellness initiatives. This includes routine administration of the **Pennsylvania Youth Survey (PAYS)** to track risk and protective factors at the population level. Building-level wellness teams report quantitative outcomes on implemented strategies. The use of behavioral screeners and the SAP process implies a data-collection phase involving teacher and parent input. While the content does not specify the use of routine progress monitoring tools like the PHQ-9 or GAD-7 for individual intervention tracking, the district's approach is informed by annual survey data, screening results, and the structured, team-based problem-solving model of SAP and MTSS.

## Technology, Data Systems & Digital Infrastructure

The Downingtown Area School District (DASD) maintains a mature and expansive digital ecosystem, anchored by a comprehensive 1:1 device program and a clearly defined core technology stack. The district's technology division prioritizes service in support of teaching and learning, managing a complex array of systems for instruction, assessment, communication, and operations. A significant emphasis is placed on student safety, data privacy, and providing unified access points for students, staff, and families.

### Core Systems & Data Architecture

The district's operational and instructional data backbone is built upon two primary platforms. **Infinite Campus** serves as the official Student Information System (SIS), described as the "secure database that maintains all student information for accountability purposes." It is the sole authoritative source for demographics, grades, attendance, discipline, class scheduling, official report cards, and transcripts. All state and federal reporting is generated from this system. The **Schoology** platform functions as the district's Learning Management System (LMS), providing a secure online classroom environment for digital content, lessons, assignments, and projects. The district explicitly cautions that any grades or attendance data posted within Schoology are "not authoritative," directing all users to Infinite Campus for official records. This delineation creates a clear, if bifurcated, data flow: instructional activities and materials reside in Schoology, while official academic records are managed in Infinite Campus.

Data integration and user access are facilitated through a single sign-on (SSO) solution. **ClassLink** is the primary SSO portal for students, allowing them to access "all their digital learning resources quickly and easily with just one set of login credentials." A separate **ClassLink portal for parents** provides a unified launchpad for family-facing applications like Infinite Campus, Schoology, and payment systems. The district also mentions a "lesser-used Clever integration tool," indicating a potential secondary or legacy integration pathway. For staff, technology support is centralized through the **FreshService** service desk platform, which manages tickets for technology issues, transportation requests, and print shop jobs, having replaced a prior system called Agiloft.

### 1:1 Device Program & Student Safety

DASD operates a district-wide 1:1 technology initiative for all students in grades K-12, with devices intended for use both at school and at home. The device strategy is differentiated by grade band: students in grades K-8 are issued a Lenovo 300e Yoga two-in-one Chromebook, while high school students (grades 9-12) receive a Lenovo ThinkPad L13 Yoga Windows two-in-one laptop. The program is supported by a mandatory technology insurance fee, structured at $50 per student in grades 1-8 and $75 per high school student (optional for kindergarten). Families qualifying for free and reduced-price lunches have this fee waived, and an annual family cap of $175 is applied. The insurance covers repairs, warranty services, loaner devices, and includes a charger and protective case.

Student safety and device management are governed by the **Securly** ecosystem. All district-issued devices have internet activity filtered and monitored by Securly regardless of physical location. The suite includes several components: **Securly** for content filtering and monitoring, which generates safety alerts for at-risk behaviors; the **Securly Home app** for parents, which allows for site blocking (e.g., YouTube), schedule creation, and real-time activity viewing; **Securly Classroom** for teacher management of Chromebooks during class; and **Securly Pass**, a cloud-based digital hall pass system for grades 6-12 (excluding the STEM Academy). Academic integrity for digital assessments is supported by **Respondus** lockdown browser and monitor software.

### Communication & Family Engagement

The district employs a multi-channel communication strategy to engage families. **Blackboard** is the mass notification platform for district- or school-level broadcasts sent via text, email, or voice in the user's language of choice. For more granular, teacher- or activity-level communication, **Remind** is used for on-demand notifications related to athletics, clubs, meetings, and field trips. At the elementary level (K-5), **Class Dojo** supports teacher-family collaboration focused on social-emotional learning within the MTSS/PBIS framework. **PickUp Patrol** is used specifically to streamline dismissal communication in K-8 schools. For financial transactions, including technology insurance and lunch payments, the district uses **PaySchools Central** as its secure online payment portal. Parent-teacher conferences are scheduled via **PTCFast**.

### Data Flow and Silo Analysis

The district's technology landscape, while well-documented, presents a classic case of platform proliferation, particularly in the areas of assessment and intervention. The explicit separation between the instructional LMS (Schoology) and the official SIS (Infinite Campus) necessitates manual or batch-grade transfer processes, creating a potential point of friction and data latency. Furthermore, the district employs a high number of point solutions for specific functions: **i-Ready** for universal screening and personalized learning, **Class Dojo** for K-5 behavior tracking (linked to PBIS), **IEP Writer** for special education documentation, **Securly** for safety monitoring, and **Signs of Suicide** for mental health screening. There is no indication in the provided content of a centralized data warehouse or dashboard that unifies these disparate streams for a holistic view of student performance, behavior, and wellness. The **ClassLink** SSO provides access unification but does not imply data integration between the underlying applications.

### IT Support and Infrastructure

The Technology Division's objective is "to provide superior service in support of teaching and learning." Staff and students in grades 9-12 utilize a Single Sign-On (SSO) system for credentials, with password reset directions and support managed through the **FreshService** portal. While the content mentions network drives and infrastructure imagery (e.g., fiber optics, interactive projectors), it does not provide specific details on network upgrades, hardware refresh cycles, or detailed IT team structure beyond the existence of the service desk.

### Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Adaptive Learning

The district's approach to AI is currently implicit rather than explicitly governed by a dedicated policy. AI-driven functionality is embedded within several adopted platforms. Most notably, **ALEKS** is described as an "artificially intelligent learning and assessment system" for mathematics. **i-Ready** utilizes adaptive diagnostics to personalize instruction. Other tools like **Membean** and **Lalilo** employ adaptive, personalized learning pathways. The inclusion of **Google Workspace for Education**, noted as including "AI tools," suggests sanctioned use of generative AI features within that ecosystem. However, there is no mention of a district AI task force, usage guidelines, or approved/blocked AI tool lists, indicating that AI governance may be handled at the platform level rather than through a overarching district framework.

## Budget, Procurement & Funding

The Downingtown Area School District (DASD) operates with a highly structured and award-winning procurement function, guided by a philosophy of maximizing taxpayer value while maintaining high-quality goods and services. The district’s fiscal operations are managed by a dedicated Business Office led by Director of Finance & Operations Bryan Howett, with a specific Procurement Manager, Matthew House, overseeing all purchasing activities. DASD has received significant recognition for its procurement excellence, including the **Gold Award of Excellence in School Purchasing Operations** from the Pennsylvania Association of School Business Officials (PASBO) in 2017 and 2020, making it the only district in Pennsylvania to receive the gold award twice. The district also notes a history of receiving the National Association of School Business Officials Meritorious Budget award for 19 consecutive years and has maintained a AAA financial rating for 16 years.

### Procurement Process & Thresholds
The district’s procurement is strictly governed by Pennsylvania School Code and internal board policies. The purchasing department reviews and approves all purchase orders. Specific monetary thresholds dictate the required competitive process. For purchases between **$12,900 and $23,800**, the district must obtain three quotations unless buying from an “approved” vendor (a member of a legal purchasing consortium). For purchases over **$23,800**, public advertising and bidding are required, again unless using an approved vendor. Notably, the Downingtown Board of School Directors has imposed an internal policy that is more stringent than state code: three quotes must be obtained for **all purchases over $5,000**. A critical rule emphasizes that these thresholds apply to the *district-wide, annual aggregate spend* for predictable, like items (e.g., copy paper, technology), not just individual transactions.

### Consortium Memberships & Approved Vendors
DASD heavily leverages cooperative purchasing to streamline procurement and ensure compliance. The district is an active member of the **Chester County Intermediate Unit (CCIU) Joint Purchasing Consortium**, which conducts bids for items like copy paper, athletic supplies, custodial supplies, mulch, and fuel. The CCIU also provides approved vendor discount catalogs that serve as the primary ordering source for many categories. Beyond the CCIU, DASD participates in a wide array of other approved purchasing consortiums and contracts. These include state programs like **PA State Contract**, **COSTARS** (Commonwealth of Pennsylvania’s cooperative purchasing program), and DGS contracts. It also utilizes contracts from the Association of Educational Purchasing Agencies (AEPA), such as **KPN**, **PAEJPC**, **PEPPM**, **NJPA**, and **TCPN**. National contracts like **US Communities** and the **GSA Technology Schedule 70** are also approved sources. For general office supplies, the district uses Office Depot as its primary vendor under the TCPN contract, noting that online ordering through such partners has proven time and cost-effective, leading the district to no longer participate in line-item bidding for general school/office supplies.

The district maintains an active and public procurement calendar, with several recent and upcoming solicitations focused on technology infrastructure, facilities, and food service equipment. A major upcoming project is the **Downingtown Area School District Internal Fiber Optic Wiring Upgrade Project Bid – High Schools**, with a mandatory pre-bid meeting on **Tuesday, March 31, 2026**, and a bid submission deadline of **9:00 AM on Thursday, April 9, 2026**. Despite these pressures, DASD emphasizes its historically frugal management, noting it has one of the lowest per-pupil instructional expenses in Chester County and maintained a zero percent tax increase in eight of the past eleven years. ## Community, News & Strategic Direction

The Downingtown Area School District (DASD) is navigating its future with a clear, community-informed strategic vision, a strong emphasis on student belonging and mental health, and a proactive stance on academic excellence and fiscal stewardship. The district’s trajectory is defined by the implementation of a detailed six-year strategic plan, significant investments in student support systems, and active efforts to engage families and recapture students from charter schools.

### Strategic Vision and Planning
The district’s direction is formally guided by a six-year strategic plan developed during the 2023-24 school year in partnership with **Performance Fact** and involving over 300 stakeholders, including students, staff, families, and community leaders. This evidence-based plan serves as the district’s roadmap, with annual reviews to align priorities with evolving needs. The plan is organized around four overarching goals that frame all district initiatives: **Goal 1: Students feel safe, connected and engaged**; **Goal 2: Students achieve core academic standards & skills**; **Goal 3: Students experience growth through equitable support**; and **Goal 4: Students are prepared for a future of their choice**. These goals are supported by foundational "pillars" and a **Portrait of a Graduate**, which articulates the community's aspirations for student success beyond graduation. For the 2025-26 school year, district initiatives are explicitly framed within these strategic goals, alongside key focuses on fiscal stewardship and facilities, indicating a disciplined approach to aligning daily operations with long-term objectives.

### Board Governance and Strategic Oversight
The Board of School Directors operates through a **Committee of the Whole** structure, meeting on the first Wednesday of each month for discussion, with formal action taken at the monthly Board meeting on the second Wednesday. This process emphasizes transparency and community input, with meetings live-streamed and archived. The board has established dedicated committees including Curriculum, Facilities, Finance, Personnel, Policy, Technology, and notably, a **Student Life Committee**, chaired by Director Madhu Gurthy. This committee’s existence signals the board’s institutional focus on the climate, culture, and non-academic supports central to Strategic Goal 1. The board utilizes **BoardDocs** for agenda and policy management, with a public process for 30-day reviews of proposed policies.

### Deep Investment in Student Well-being and Systemic Supports
A dominant theme in the district's recent activity is a comprehensive, data-driven approach to student mental health, belonging, and behavior support. This is a core component of Strategic Goal 3 (equitable support) and is evidenced by a series of concrete actions and partnerships.
The district has implemented universal social-emotional and mental health screening using the **BESS (Behavioral and Emotional Screening System)** screener for grades 3-9. A significant development in the 2025-26 year is the formation of a "universal screening committee to look at alternatives to the BESS," indicating an ongoing evaluation and refinement of their assessment tools. This data is actively used to "create and implement interventions." The district participates in the **Pennsylvania Youth Survey (PAYS)** and a **Pennsylvania Climate Survey** to gather broader data on student experiences and school environment.
To act on this data, DASD has established multiple formal partnerships with external agencies to embed support within schools. These include:
*   **Devereux**: Providing support for **PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports)**, annual **TFI (Tiered Fidelity Inventory)** completion, behavioral health liaisons at the elementary level, and SAP (Student Assistance Program) support at specific middle schools.
*   **Caron Treatment Centers**: Providing behavioral health liaisons at the high schools for SAP and delivering prevention programs like "Braindrain" and "Clearfacts."
*   **Holcomb Behavioral Health**: Partners in a mental health collaborative focused on leveraging social-emotional data to support Tier I and II programming.
*   **Cartwheel**: Providing virtual mental health support.
The district also reports implementing **Second Step SEL curriculum** and achieving recognition for its PBIS implementation, with several schools earning Tier 1, 2, and 3 recognition from PaPBS. Furthermore, a dedicated **Student Life Committee** meets monthly at all secondary schools, and the district has launched initiatives like a "Belongingness Toolkit" and "Making Connections" programs with local police to foster student engagement and safety. These efforts have yielded tangible outcomes; the district notes a **63% decrease in habitual truancy** and removal from a "Significant Disproportionality risk threshold."

### Academic Program Development and Curriculum Modernization
The district is concurrently engaged in a refresh of its academic core, aligning with Strategic Goal 2. Key curriculum actions include the full implementation of **iReady** as a K-8 math core resource and diagnostic tool, and the piloting of **Amplify** resources for 6-8 ELA. A K-5 math **Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS)** has been fully implemented across all ten elementary schools, supported by the addition of four math specialists at Title I schools. The district is also developing K-6 homework guidelines and engaging in "monthly K-6 administrative team classroom walkthroughs" to calibrate instructional practices. A major operational shift is the **transition from Illuminate to Enterprise Reporting for a districtwide assessment warehouse**, aimed at improving data accessibility and the implementation of **Early Warning System Reports in Infinite Campus**. The launch of a **full-day kindergarten program** in the 2025-26 school year represents a significant expansion of early learning services.

A notable strategic communications and recruitment effort is the district’s **"School of Choice"** initiative, which explicitly encourages families to choose DASD over charter and cyber charter schools. The campaign, championed by Superintendent Robert J. O'Donnell, cites data that DASD students outperform county charter school peers by an average of 44% in math and 37% in ELA. The initiative openly discusses the financial impact of charter school tuition on the district while showcasing testimonials from 127 families who returned to DASD, praising the welcoming environment, academic rigor, and support services. ### Operational and Fiscal Stewardship
The district publicly emphasizes responsible fiscal management, listing it as a key initiative area alongside educational goals. While specific budget details are not in the provided content, the mention of the **"2025-26 Budget"** as a dedicated initiative area confirms the ongoing budget cycle and planning process. The district also manages **A Child's Place**, a fee-based before and after-school care program in all ten elementary schools, which represents an auxiliary service supporting family needs.

### Portrait of a Graduate and Future Readiness
Strategic Goal 4 focuses on preparing students for the "four Es": Education, Employment, Enlistment, and Entrepreneurship. In the 2025-26 school year, the district has created a **Portrait of a Graduate Taskforce** to operationalize this vision.
